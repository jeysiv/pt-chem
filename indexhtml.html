Introduction

STOICHIOMETRY

        In Greek, the word stoichiometry means "measuring elements." It is a branch of chemistry that involves the calculation of reactants and products in chemical reactions. It allows us to count atoms and molecules by weighing them. It provides systematic way to predict the outcomes of chemical reactions and is fundamental for various practical applications in chemistry and related fields.

History of stoichiometry

- Law of Conservation of Mass (18th century) established by Antoine Lavoiser deomsntrated that in a chemical reaction, mass is neither created nor destoyed, which is the basis for balancing chemical equations.

- Laws of Definite Proportion (1797) Joseph Proust formulated this law, which states that a chemical compound always contains its component elements in a fixed ratio by mass, a key principle in stoichiometry, as it established that proportions of elements in compound are consistent and predictable.

- Dalton's Atomic Theory and the Law of Multiple Proportions (1803) by John Dalton introduced his Atomic Theory, which proposed that elements are made of tiny, indivisible particles called atoms, and that atoms combine in simple whole-number ratios to form compounds.

- Avogadro's Hypothesis (1811) by Amedeo Avogadro proposedthat equal volumes of gases, the same temperature and pressure, contain an equal number of particles (later called molecules).

- Molecular and Atomic Weights (19th century). This is where the Periodic Law by Dmitri Mendeleev in 1869 was introduced, facilitating more precise stoichiometric equations.

Concepts

ATOMIC MASS

        It is the average mass of an atom of an element, expressed in atomic mass units (amu). It is the quantity of matter contained in an atom of an element, and is not the same as atomic weight. Atomic mass is the weighted average of the masses of all the isotopes of an element. This means that the atomic mass takes into account the relative abundance of each isotope in nature.

For example:

Carbon - 12 has an atomic mass of 12 amu, and it makes up about 98.9% of all carbon atoms.

Carbon - 14 has an atomic mass of 14 amu, and it makes up about 1.1% of all carbon atoms.

It is represented by the symbol NA, and its value is approximately 6.02214076 x 10^23 particles per mole. MOLE CONCEPT

       The mole concept is a fundamental idea in chemistry that allows us to count atoms and molecules in a measurable way. The mole concept bridges the microscopic world of atoms with the macroscopic quantities we observe in the lab. By understanding the mole, we can calculate the amounts of substances needed or produced in chemical reactions.


What is a Mole in Chemistry?

        A mole is a unit of measurement used in chemistry to express the amount of a chemical substance. It is equivalent to particles, which could be atoms, molecules, or ions. This value is known as Avogadro's number. Just like we use "dozen" to count 12 items, chemists use "mole" to count incredibly tiny particles like atoms and molecules in larger, measureble quantities. The mole became an essential tool in chemistry, allowing for precise measurements of chemical reactions and the relationships between reactants and products.


Using moles in chemistry

       The mole concept plays a vital role in converting between different units in chemistry. Here are some common conversions:

      Mole-to-Particles Conversion: To convert moles to particles (atoms, molecules, etc.), you simply multiply the number of moles by Avogadro's number. For example:

                1 mole of any substance = particles
                 If you have 2 moles of hydrogen gas, that's molecules of hydrogen.


        Mole-to-Mass Conversion: To convert moles to mass, you multiply the number of moles by molar mass (in grams per mole) of the substance. For example:

                 The molar mass of water (H2O) is 18 grams per mole. So, 2 moles of water would have a mass of grams.

CHEMICAL FORMULA

        A chemical formula is a formula that tells how many atoms of a particular element, or multiple elements, are found within a certain chemical compound. The numbers that appear as subscripts on certain symbols within the formula are used to represent how many of each particular atom are found within the chemical substance. For instance, the chemical formula for water is H2O. The subscript on the symbol of hydrogen is two, which means there are two hydrogen atoms in water. The oxygen atom lacks a subscript, as there is only one atom of oxygen in the chemical formula for water.


            There are two types of commonly observed formulas in chemistry.

- Empirical Formula
- Molecular Formula

EMPIRICAL FORMULA

        The word empirical is defined as something that is verified by observation. In chemistry, the empirical formula is defined as the simplest ration of whole numbers of elements that make up a compound, and this type of formula is derived from experimental data.

CHEMICAL REACTIONS

        What happens to matter when it undergoes chemical changes? The Law of Conservation of mass says that "Atoms are neither created, nor destroyed, during any chemical reaction." Thus, the same collection of atoms is present after a reaction as before the reaction. The changes that occur during a reaction just involve the rearrangement of atoms.



        Chemical reactions are represented on paper by chemical equations. For example, hydrogen gas (H2) can react (burn) with oxygen gas (O2) to form water (H2O). The chemical equation for this reaction for this reaction is written as:


2H2 + O2 → 2H2O



        Chemical formulas and other symbols are used to indicate the starting materials, or reactants, which by convention are written on the left side of the equation, and the final compounds, or products, which are written on the right. An arrow points from the reactant to the products. The arrow is read as "yields" or "reacts to form."

BALANCING SIMPLE CHEMICAL EQUATIONS

        When a chemist encounters a new reaction, it does not usually come with a label that shows the balanced chemical equation. Instead, the chemist must identify the reactants and products and then write them in the form of a chemical equation that may or may not be balanced as first written. Consider, for example, the combustion of n-heptane (C7H16), an important component of gasoline.


C7H16(I) + O2(g) → CO2(g) + H2O(g)


        (The complete combustion of any hydrocarbon with sufficient oxygen always yields carbon dioxide and water.)




       The simplest and most generally useful method for balancing chemical equations is "inspection," better known as trial and error. The following is an efficient approach to balancing a chemical equation using this method.

                       Example: Combustion of Heptane


1. Identify the most complex substance. The most complex substance is the one with the largest number of different atoms, which is C7H16. We will assume initially that the final balanced chemical equation contains 1 molecule or formula of this substance.

2. Adjust coefficients. Try to adjust the coefficients of the molecules on the other side of the equation to obtain the same numbers of atoms on both sides. Because one molecule of n-heptane contains 7 carbon atoms, we need 7 carbon dioxide (CO2) molecules, each of which contains 1 carbon atom, on the right side:


C7H16 + O2 → 7CO2 + H2O



3. Balance polyatomic ions as a unit. There are no polyatomic ions to be considered in this reaction.

4. Balance the remaining atoms. Because one molecule of n-heptane contains 16 hydrogen atoms, we need 8 water (H2O) molecules, each of which contains 2 hydrogen atoms on the right side:

C7H16 + O2 → 7CO2 + 8H2O



(The carbon and hydrogen atoms are now balanced, but we have 22 oxygen atoms on the right side and only 2 oxygen atoms on the left. We can balance the oxygen atoms by adjusting the coefficient in front of the least complex substance, O2, on the reactant side:)


C7H16(I) + 11O2(g) → 7CO2(g) + 8H2O(g)



5. Check your work. The equation is now balanced, and there are no fractional coefficients: there are 7 carbon atoms, 16 hydrogen atoms, and 22 oxygen atoms on each side. Always check to be sure that a chemical equation is balanced.

TYPES OF CHEMICAL REACTIONS IN AQUEOUS SOLUTIONS

        Chemical reactions are a part of our daily lives, whether we notice them or not. From cleaning our homes to rust forming on metal objects, these reactions often happen in water-based solutions. Here's a breakdown of some common types of reactions using chemical compositions you encounter in everyday situations.


1. PRECIPITATION REACTIONS

                A precipitation reaction happens when two solutions mix and create a solid that wasn't there before. For example, when calcium ions (Ca2+) in hard water react with fatty acids from soap (C1 7H3 5COO-), they create an insoluble solid-soap scum:

Ca2+ + 2C1 7H3 5COO- → Ca(C1 7H3 5COO)2




2. ACID-BASE REACTIONS (NEUTRALIZING REACTIONS)

                Acid-base reactions are easy to spot in the kitchen. One popular example is mixing baking soda (NaHCO3) and vinegar (CH3 COOH). It bubbles and fizzes, neutralizing the acid with the base, which is why we sometimes use it for cleaning. For example, when sodium bicarbonate (NaHCO3) reacts with acetic acid (CH3 COOH), carbon dioxide gas (those bubbles), water, and sodium acetate are produced:

NaHCO3 + CH3 COOH → CO2 + H2 O + CH3 COONa




3. REDOX REACTIONS (OXIDATION-REDUCTION REACTIONS)

                Redox reactions involve the transfer of electrons, and rusting is a perfect example. If you leave something made of iron (Fe) out in the rain, it reacts with oxygen (O2) from the air, and over time, it turns into rust. For example, when iron (Fe) reacts with oxygen (O) and water (H2O), iron oxide (rust) forms:

4Fe + 3O2 + 6H2O → Fe2 O3 ·nH2O




4. DOUBLE DISPLACEMENT REACTIONS

                double displacement reactions occur when two compounds swap ions. Ever mixed lemon juice (H3 C6 H5 O7) and baking soda (NaHCO3)? The fizz you see is a result of this kind of reactions, where carbon dioxide gas is released. For example, citric acid from lemon juice reacts with sodium bicarbonate to form carbon dioxide bubbles, water, and socium citrate:

3NaHCO3 + H3 C6 H5 O7 → 3CO2 3H2O + Na3 C6 H5 O7




5. GAS-FORMING REACTIONS

                Gas-forming reactions are the kind that produce a gas as part of the reaction. Think of what happens when you drop an antacid tablet (CaCO3) into water with hydrolic acid (HCl), it fizzes, releasing carbon dioxide gas. For example, calcium carbonate in the antacid reacts with stomach acid (hydrochloric acid) to produce carbon dioxide gas, water, and calcium chloride:

CaCO3 + 2HCl → CO2 + H2O + CaCl2




6. COMBUSTION REACTIONS

                Combustion reactions involves something burning. Whether it's lighting a gas stove or starting a car, combustion is all around us. In your kitchen, when methane (CH4), the main component of natural gas, burns in oxygen (O2), it produces carbon dioxide and water vapor. For example, when methane (CH4) reacts with oxygen (O2), it produces carbon dioxide and water, along with heat:

(CH4) + 2O2 → CO2 + 2H2O
